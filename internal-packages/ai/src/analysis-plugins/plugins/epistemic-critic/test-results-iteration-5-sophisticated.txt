--- ERROR 1 ---
Header: Logical fallacy detected

Description:
**Issue Type:** Logical Fallacy

**Text:** "90% of millionaires who used our strategy achieved their wealth within 5 years"

**Analysis:** Classic survivorship bias. This only examines millionaires (successful outcomes) who used the strategy, completely ignoring all the people who used the strategy and didn't become millionaires. This is like saying '90% of lottery winners bought tickets at gas stations' - it tells us nothing about the success rate of the strategy itself. The relevant statistic would be: 'Of all people who used our strategy, what percentage became millionaires?'

**Suggested Context/Correction:**
Need to know: Of ALL clients who used this strategy, what percentage became millionaires? What's the base failure rate? How does this compare to the general population's rate of becoming millionaires?

**Research Findings:**
Survivorship bias in investment marketing claims refers to the tendency of firms to highlight only the successful funds, stocks, or strategies while omitting those that failed or were discontinued. This creates a misleading impression of performance and success rates, as the marketing materials often showcase only the 'survivors'—those investments that have performed well and remained in existence. By excluding the failures, such as funds that were closed, merged, or went bankrupt, the overall picture presented to investors is overly optimistic and can lead to poor decision-making. This bias is particularly prevalent in mutual fund marketing, where underperforming funds are removed from performance statistics, making the remaining funds appear more successful than they actually are.

**Analysis:** Multiple reasoning errors: (1) Cherry-picked timeframe - why exactly 10 years? What happened in years 11-20? Regression to the mean in stock market returns after growth periods refers to the tendency for unusually high or low returns to be followed by returns closer to the long-term average. This statistical phenomenon implies that after a period of strong growth, future returns are likely to be lower or closer to average, rather than continuing at the extreme level. However, while this concept is widely recognized in investing, the evidence for strong mean reversion in stock returns over short horizons is weak, and it is not a reliable basis for profitable market timing. Over long periods, stock market returns tend to revert to a growth trend, but deviations can persist for years or decades.

**Issue Type:** Logical Fallacy

**Text:** "We surveyed our user base and 95% reported satisfaction with their results"

To mitigate survivorship bias, organizations should gather feedback at multiple points in the customer journey, include both active and inactive customers, and triangulate survey data with behavioral and employee feedback.

**Analysis:** Absolute vs. relative risk framing manipulation. **Research Findings:**
Relative and absolute risk framing are two ways of presenting risk information, each with distinct implications for decision-making, especially in financial and health contexts. Relative risk describes the ratio of risk between two groups (e.g., '50% higher risk'), while absolute risk describes the actual difference in risk (e.g., 'risk increases from 2% to 3%'). In financial claims, relative risk framing can exaggerate the perceived impact of a risk or benefit, potentially misleading consumers. Absolute risk framing provides a clearer picture of the actual change, helping people make more informed decisions. Studies show that people are more likely to choose optimal options when risks are presented in absolute terms, as relative framing can lead to cognitive biases and overestimation of risk or benefit.
